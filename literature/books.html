My book recommendations

Some of my favorite books! Many of these descriptions were written circa 2022 so sorry if they are awkward.
On the Road — Jack Kerouac
If you are in a transition period in your life I think this is the book to read. Notoriously hard to describe, but know it's famous for a reason. I think this book had a really big impact on me and I'm grateful I read it at the time I did (ending HS and going into college).
The Remains of the Day — Kazuo Ishiguro
A story of duty and regret.
5 Stories of Music and Nightfall — Kazuo Ishiguro
Just as beautiful as The Remains of the Day. Ishiguro's writing is like the sound of silence.
Crime and Punishment — Fyodor Dostoevsky
So complex, so good, oh my gosh I loved it with all my heart. A young man and his moral scruples, philosophy, struggles, etc…
No Longer Human — Osamu Dazai
A very troubled man who can’t really connect/open up/live as others do. He puts a sort of mask on himself. “I had now ceased utterly to be a human being.”
Anything by Kafka
Though my personal favorites and recommendations are: The Metamorphosis (classic), the Trial, A Hunger Artist (my favorite!), In the Penal Colony, or Poseidon. I love almost everything he writes. The one story I notably could hardly get through was The Burrow, but I think I was too young when I read it, and did not read it in the right setting.
The Stranger — Albert Camus
This book is amazing. It's beautiful. It's so good. I have read it four times and it gets better each time. I first read it in 8th grade and it's the reason I am so into literature. A good introduction to his existential philosophy, and it really makes you ponder. The way Camus writes is amazing, he’s the master at beginning a story in my opinion. Though, I think that's better said when describing A Happy Death by Camus. That is my personal favorite beginning by Camus. Also a good recommendation.
Steppenwolf — Hermann Hesse
Warning, Total oversimplification: man is depressed, and views himself as a subhuman/half-wolf being. Isolation, despair, madness, color, creativity, SO good. My dad said he knows a lot of Californians who like this book, oddly enough.
Demian — Hermann Hesse
A definite read if you know anything about Carl Jung’s “Map of the Soul.” You could psychoanalyze every detail in this book (as is common with Hesse books) but this one is pretty sick.
The Victim — Saul Bellow
Follows a kind of common trope in books (experiencing fault and guilt without justification), but I think Saul Bellow does it expertly. A really good read.
The Dangling Man — Saul Bellow
Guy wants to be drafted into the military—but the entire book just focuses on the waiting period; how he’s just dangling in life, waiting for that letter of enlistment. It’s also interesting to read and feel how his character progresses throughout the book. As a bonus, it's a killer reference to compare yourself to when you feel like you are in a waiting period.
If Beale Street Could Talk — James Baldwin
A beautiful and heartbreaking love story set in Harlem. Baldwin's prose is lyrical and vibrant, and he portrays a heartbreaking story of love, injustice, and racism.
Go Tell It On the Mountain — James Baldwin
About a boy and his relationship with himself, his family, and the church!!! It's so good but also sad!
ANYTHING by James Baldwin honestly
He is genuinely one of the best authors to have ever existed. His stories are poignant, emotional, gut-wrenching, and absolutely beautiful. If there is anything you read from this page let it be a James Baldwin book. I am not good at writing descriptions of writing styles, so the best way I can describe his writing is: rather than the words being printed onto paper, they read as if Baldwin carved them into the book with a knife, and now the pages are bleeding out. Truly one of the best authors.
